Twitter Data Scraping (Python)

Exploring public discourse using texting mining & data visualization

The Challenge

This research project explored how sentiment toward climate change is expressed on Twitter and uncovered dominant themes within this discourse. Analyzing a dataset of top tweets from January to July 2022, I applied sentiment analysis and topic modeling techniques to understand the digital climate conversation. The goal was to identify public emotions and reveal how online users frame the climate crisis using both data-driven and qualitative methods.

Methods & Approach

The dataset, collected from Kaggle, consisted of 9,050 tweets containing the keyword "climate change." Each tweet included metrics like likes, retweets, and sentiment scores. My methods combined:

  • Text Preprocessing: Tokenization, lemmatization, and stop word removal using Python.
  • Sentiment Analysis: VADER tool for social media sentiment classification.
  • Topic Modeling: Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to extract key discussion topics.
  • Data Visualization: Created word clouds, bar graphs, and word embeddings to visually convey results.
  • Qualitative Coding: Interpreted dominant themes and categorized tweets by emotional framing.

This mix of computational and qualitative tools helped uncover nuanced themes and emotion-driven patterns in climate communication.

Findings

Sentiment was nearly evenly split among the tweets: 36% positive, 35% negative, and 29% neutral. Word clouds revealed themes like "support," "action," and "renewable" under positive sentiment, while "crisis," "threat," and "disaster" dominated negative sentiment.

Topic modeling showed:

  • Negative tweets emphasized global crisis narratives and dissatisfaction with government action
  • Neutral tweets included academic discussions and reporting
  • Positive tweets promoted solutions, community efforts, and innovation

Word2Vec embeddings demonstrated contrasting word associations—while "crisis" was paired with urgency-driven terms, "prosperity" linked with growth, inclusion, and sustainability. These findings illustrate a complex emotional and thematic landscape in climate communication on Twitter.
